Cryptocurrencies and Bitcoin, the environmental problem of cryptocurrencies



Created from a 'blockchain' network (a system that allows the sending and receiving of some types of information over the internet, acting as a large database in which pieces of code carry information), cryptocurrencies is a generic name given to digitized and decentralized “mined” coins from advanced cryptographic systems.


Unlike currencies such as the Dollar, the Real or the Libra, cryptocurrencies only exist on the internet, and, as much as their veracity is verified, are impossible to physically store. Furthermore, transactions using cryptocurrencies are not mediated by a regulatory organization, being the sole responsibility of the user.

Because they are “mined”, that is, several “miners” try to find solutions to complex equations in order to validate the data of the blockchain, for this to occur, computers with high processing capacity are needed, larger than common computers; at the same time, energy consumption as well. According to an analysis carried out by the University of Cambridge, in the United Kingdom, currently the cryptocurrency validation and mining consumes about 130.9 terawatts/hour per year, for comparative purposes, the country of Argentina consumes about 125 terawatt hours per year. It is estimated that mining will be responsible for the emission (direct and indirect) of approximately 20 million tons of CO2 by 2025.

Regarding the strong environmental impact that the cryptocurrency represents, together with its great financial potential and the perspective, not only of valorization, but also of investment, alternative forms of use and emissions of energy can be explored in order to alleviate the environmental effects.

An alternative for the reduction of energy consumed would be renewable energy sources such as wind energy and the physic approximation of energy sources, so there is less energy dissipation because this energy is transmitted over a shorter distance.
